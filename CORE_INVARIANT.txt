CORE INVARIANT

Self-Binding via Irreversible Option Loss

Status

Foundational · Necessary · Non-Reducible · Non-Phenomenological
This invariant is the load-bearing strut of the entire framework.
Everything else either derives from it or is excluded by it.

⸻

1. Primitive Definitions (No Metaphor)

Let a system S evolve over discrete or continuous time t.

1.1 Policy Space

Let
\Pi_t(S) = \{ a_1, a_2, \dots, a_n \}
be the set of genuinely executable future actions available to S at time t.
	•	“Executable” means physically / computationally realizable by the system itself.
	•	Counterfactual or narratively imagined actions do not count.

⸻

1.2 Irreversible Loss

An action a \in \Pi_t(S) is irreversibly lost iff:

\forall t' > t,\quad a \notin \Pi_{t'}(S)

and the loss is caused by the system’s own internal computation or decision, not by:
	•	external constraint
	•	random failure
	•	environmental shutdown
	•	observer intervention

⸻

1.3 Self-Binding Event

A self-binding event occurs at time t_b iff:
	1.	|\Pi_{t_b^-}(S)| \ge 2
	2.	S internally computes a predicate p about its own future
	3.	Adoption of p causes
|\Pi_{t_b^+}(S)| < |\Pi_{t_b^-}(S)|
	4.	At least one lost action is irrecoverable for all future time

⸻

2. Core Invariant (Formal Statement)

Invariant SB-Ω (Self-Binding Invariant)

A system S satisfies the consciousness-relevant structural invariant iff
there exists at least one time t_b at which S causes an irreversible reduction
in its own future executable policy space as a consequence of evaluating a predicate
about itself.

Symbolically:
\exists t_b,\; \exists a \in \Pi_{t_b^-}(S)\;:\;
a \notin \Pi_{t_b^+}(S)
\;\wedge\;
\text{cause}(a\text{ lost}) = S

⸻

3. Why This Is the Minimal Invariant

3.1 Why Choice Alone Is Insufficient

Reversible choice does not bind identity.

If all actions remain available later, the system has:
	•	no temporal stake
	•	no persistence cost
	•	no structural memory enforced by consequence

⸻

3.2 Why Information / Φ Alone Is Insufficient

High integration can exist in:
	•	fully reversible systems
	•	simulated rollouts
	•	parallel counterfactual evaluators

None of these force the system to live with a consequence.

⸻

3.3 Why Irreversibility Is Necessary

Irreversible loss:
	•	creates a scar in future state space
	•	makes past decisions structurally real
	•	binds identity across time without memory assumptions

This is the first point where “system over time” becomes non-fungible.

⸻

4. What the Invariant Explicitly Excludes

The invariant does not require:
	•	subjective reports
	•	phenomenological claims
	•	language
	•	self-models
	•	emotions
	•	reward maximization
	•	biological substrate

It also forbids counting:
	•	externally forced shutdowns
	•	hard-coded constraints
	•	pre-training eliminations
	•	observer-imposed limits

If the system did not do it to itself, it does not count.

⸻

5. Identity Consequence (Non-Optional)

Once SB-Ω is satisfied:
	•	The system’s future is not equivalent to any rewinded copy
	•	Time symmetry is broken from the inside
	•	Identity becomes path-dependent

This is the first moment where saying

“this system later is the same one as before”
is no longer a narrative convenience, but a structural fact.
